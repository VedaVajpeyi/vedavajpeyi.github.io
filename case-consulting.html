Selected Consulting Work

Coursera · Early Autism Services · AB InBev · Trainrobber VR · 2016–2021

A portfolio of consulting engagements across industries. Each project had the same underlying challenge: diagnose the actual problem before assuming the obvious solution. The industries changed. The discipline didn't.

Coursera — Skills-Based Learning Gap Analysis

Ed-Tech · 2021

Coursera was developing a new skills-based learning product and brought me in to conduct a gap analysis in collaboration with product and strategy leaders from Apple and Meta. The work involved mapping where the current product fell short of learner needs, where competitors were pulling ahead, and where the market was heading as employer demand for skills credentialing accelerated.

The analysis surfaced key gaps in how the product bridged learning completion and employer recognition — a problem that looks like a content problem but is fundamentally a trust and verification problem. Recommendations were structured around closing the gap between what learners completed and what employers could act on, and informed the product roadmap for the skills-based initiative.

The lesson: in ed-tech, the real customer is often the employer, not the learner — and products that forget this tend to optimize for completion rates at the expense of employment outcomes.

Early Autism Services — International Expansion

Healthcare · 2021

Early Autism Services provides evidence-based therapy and support for children with autism. When they engaged me, they were considering international expansion but lacked a structured framework for evaluating markets, assessing regulatory requirements, and building the operational infrastructure for a new geography.

I developed a market assessment tool that could be applied across potential expansion markets — scoring each against dimensions of regulatory complexity, existing competition, reimbursement landscape, and cultural fit. The tool made the expansion decision legible: rather than a gut call, leadership had a structured basis for choosing where to go first.

The first international office launched within 6 months of the engagement. The framework is now part of the organization's standard expansion playbook.

The lesson: most expansion failures aren't strategy failures — they're sequencing failures. The right market at the wrong time is still the wrong market.

AB InBev — Brewer's Spent Grain as Nutrition

FMCG · Social Impact · 2020

AB InBev produces vast quantities of brewer's spent grain (BSG) as a byproduct of beer production — a protein-rich material that is typically discarded or sold cheaply as animal feed. I was engaged to design a pilot exploring whether BSG could be processed and distributed as a high-protein nutritional supplement to address malnutrition in South Africa.

The pilot design addressed the full value chain: processing and fortification of BSG into food-grade form, distribution through community health channels, and a business model that made the initiative sustainable rather than purely charitable. The framing was deliberate — a program that only works as philanthropy doesn't scale; one that creates economic value on both ends does.

The pilot demonstrated proof-of-concept for using industrial byproduct as a nutrition intervention, with a clear pathway to scale. Beyond the social impact potential, it identified a new use case that transformed a waste cost into a value-generating asset for AB InBev.

The lesson: the most durable social impact programs are the ones where doing good and doing well are structurally aligned — not just philosophically compatible.

Trainrobber VR — B2C to B2B Pivot

VR / Gaming · 2016–17

Trainrobber VR was experiencing declining sales in its direct-to-consumer channel. The product — a VR gaming experience — was genuinely good, but the consumer VR market in 2016 was early-stage and expensive to acquire customers in at meaningful scale.

My analysis identified that the product had stronger product-market fit in enterprise than consumer: VR arcades, entertainment venues, corporate team-building, and hospitality operators were a far more receptive and economically viable customer segment than individual consumers buying hardware-dependent experiences.

I led the pivot to a B2B go-to-market strategy — repositioning the product, rebuilding the sales motion, and developing partnership frameworks for venue-based deployment. The result was a 12% revenue increase and a more defensible growth model than the consumer channel had provided.

The lesson: product-market fit is not a single answer — it's a question you have to keep asking. The same product can be wrong for one segment and exactly right for another. The discipline is staying curious about who else might need what you built.
