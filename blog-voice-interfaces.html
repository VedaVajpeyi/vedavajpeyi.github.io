What Voice Interfaces Teach Us About Bad Product Thinking

Veda Vajpeyi · 2025 · 8 min read

Voice interfaces remove the safety net of visual design. There's no button to blame, no layout to hide friction in, no white space to mask a broken interaction. The interface IS the interaction. When someone talks to a device and it doesn't understand, or understands wrong, the entire product has failed. There's nowhere to hide.

This is a brutal constraint. And it's exactly why shipping voice products taught me more about product thinking than anything else I've done.

Most of us work in digital products where screens do a lot of heavy lifting. Screens let us defer the hardest questions. Design can make a mediocre interaction feel intentional. A confusing feature can be padded with helpful text and onboarding. A feature that doesn't quite work can be buried beneath others that do. The screen is patient. The screen is forgiving.

The Screen Is a Crutch

I spent years watching product teams make decisions I knew were wrong, but which looked defensible on a screen. The classic example: a feature that works only if you perform five specific steps in the exact right order, and remembers none of your context between sessions. On a mobile app, you design around this. You create affordances, visual hierarchies, clever microcopy. It feels intentional. It can even feel good.

Then you try to build it for voice.

Suddenly, you can't AB test the phrasing. You can't tweak the interaction order. You can't rely on visual memory. The user has to either understand what to do from your words alone, or they don't. There's no color coding, no icons, no spatial logic. Either the interaction works or it doesn't. And if it doesn't, it's not because your design team didn't have time to polish it. It's because the interaction itself is broken.

This clarity is uncomfortable. It forces you to ask: would anyone actually want this interaction in their head? Would I explain this to someone the same way I'm asking the device to understand it? If the answer is no, then the product is wrong, not the interface.

The Metric Problem Is Deeper Than You Think

Most voice engagement metrics measure volume, not value. A person who says "set a timer" ten times and gives up on timers is counted as a success. Engagement ≠ value. A user who uses a feature once successfully and never needs it again looks identical to a user who tries it repeatedly and abandons it in frustration.

This matters everywhere, but voice makes it impossible to ignore. With a screen, you can hide behind multiple metrics. Session length, repeat rate, conversion funnel drop-off. Each one tells you something, and as a team, you pick the metrics that make your feature look good. Voice stripped that away for me. I realized I couldn't even measure success without answering the first question: what is this actually supposed to do?

"The question isn't why won't people use this. The question is: what is this interface actually good for?"

When you can't hide behind design flourishes, you have to measure what matters: did it solve the problem? Did the user feel like it worked? Would they come back? These are harder questions. They require talking to people, watching them actually struggle or succeed, sitting with the discomfort of failure. But they're the only questions that actually matter.

Trust Is Not a Feature, It's Everything

With visual interfaces, trust is something you build over time. A good experience, clear design, consistency, good security practices. Trust accumulates like a bank account. You have a balance, and you withdraw from it with each bad interaction.

Voice is different. There's no bank account. There's a wire. The moment a device mishears or misunderstands in a moment that matters—setting a medical alarm, controlling a stove, calling someone by mistake—the relationship doesn't just get damaged. It breaks. Trust in voice is earned in milliseconds and lost in one bad interaction.

This is brutal feedback. It's also clarifying. It forced me to think about the core of what I was building differently. Not: how do we get people to use this more? But: how do we make this so reliable, so predictable, so aligned with what people expect, that they don't have to think about whether to trust it?

Most product teams don't ask this question. Most products fail long before they need to. The ones that work do so because they're built on an unstated contract: I will do this one thing, and I will do it reliably. No surprises. No assumptions. No attempting to be smart in ways that could go wrong.

The "Ambient" Assumption Is Wrong

Most voice products were built on a beautiful assumption: that people would naturally integrate these devices into their lives. That voice would become the primary interface for everything, because it's the most natural human interface.

The reality is narrower. People use voice for a specific set of high-frequency, low-stakes tasks. Timers. Weather. Music. Simple yes-or-no questions. Not for nuanced conversations, not for complex decisions, not for anything where being misunderstood has consequences. This wasn't a failure of implementation. It was a failure of vision.

Here's what I realized: the question isn't why won't people use this? The question is: what is this interface actually good for? Not what we want it to be good for. Not what would be cool. What are people actually comfortable delegating to a voice interface? Answer that honestly, and you have a product. Ignore it, and you have technology searching for a use case.

This reframing applies everywhere. Most product problems aren't about execution. They're about answering a question you never asked: is this what people actually need? Voice products don't let you answer with vibes or faith or hope. You have to know.

The Best Thing Voice Taught Me

What voice interfaces did for me as a PM was take away all my excuses. I couldn't blame design. I couldn't hide behind metrics. I couldn't assume people would grow into the product if it just became ambient enough. I had to answer the hard questions:

What is this for? Who actually needs it? Why would they choose this over what they do now? What could go wrong, and what happens then? How would I know if it was working?

These are the same questions I should have been asking for every product I worked on. Voice just made it impossible to ignore them. The constraints of the medium—no visual design to lean on, no design debt to defer, no way to measure anything but actual value—forced clarity.

Most teams get to choose whether to ask themselves hard questions. Voice doesn't let you choose. It just asks them, whether you're ready or not. And once you've sat with those questions, you can't unsee them. You start asking them everywhere. About every feature, every decision, every assumption you've been getting away with.

The best products aren't built on technology. They're built on a clear-eyed understanding of what people actually need, what they'll actually use, and why they'll trust you to deliver it. Voice taught me that. And it changed how I think about product, period.
