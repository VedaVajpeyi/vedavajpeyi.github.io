Making Existing Value Unmistakable

Consumer Hardware · Subscription Products · 2022–Present

Before building new value, the harder question is whether you've made the existing value unmistakable. Most teams skip it.

This is a thinking case. Specifics of the product, program names, and outcomes are confidential. The framework and the reframe are mine to share.

The Setup

Leadership identified a significant gap: a large segment of high-value customers weren't adopting a device that was designed, in part, for them. The hypothesis was that the product lacked a compelling enough reason for this segment to buy in — a missing 'silver bullet' feature.

My mandate: find it and build it.

I almost did.

Why I Didn't

Before committing to building anything, I mapped the customer journey end to end — how people discovered, considered, and used the device. The research surfaced something uncomfortable: many of the features this customer segment said they wished the device had already existed.

They just didn't know.

Customers perceived the device as a narrow-use product — useful for a small cluster of tasks — despite being capable of significantly more. The gap wasn't product capability. It was product awareness.

Leadership chased a 'silver bullet' feature because users said they wanted more. The research uncovered the gap: they already had the value — they just weren't acting on it. Closing that awareness-behavior disconnect proved far more powerful (and cheaper) than new builds.

This reframe had a direct strategic implication: building a new feature to drive adoption would have added to a set of capabilities customers were already ignoring. It would have solved the wrong problem with expensive engineering.

The Business Case for Changing Direction

Reframing the strategy required more than an insight — it required proof that the pivot was worth the organizational cost. I ran a cohort analysis to understand what happened to customers who did engage deeply with the device. The pattern was clear: higher engagement correlated strongly with retention and spend on adjacent products.

That gave me a lever. The argument was no longer 'we should fix awareness.' It was 'the upside of fixing awareness is this — and here's what it's worth.'

I brought the research, the cohort data, and a proposal to leadership: instead of a single feature, build a dedicated initiative to close the awareness gap and create tighter integration between the device and the broader ecosystem of benefits.

What This Taught Me

The most expensive product mistake isn't building the wrong thing. It's building a new thing when an existing thing isn't being seen.

Before committing to new investment, I now ask: have we made what already exists unmistakable? Not discoverable — unmistakable. The distinction matters. Discoverability is a UX problem. Unmistakability is a strategic one.
The research showed that customers wanted exactly what we'd already built. They just didn't know it existed. That's not a product gap — it's a failure of connection between value and the people it's meant for.
