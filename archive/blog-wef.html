On Education, Transformation, and What Davos Got Right

Veda Vajpeyi · World Economic Forum Annual Meeting, 2020

In early 2020, I was invited to the World Economic Forum's Annual Meeting in Davos to participate in discussions on "Education for Transformation" — specifically around the UN's Sustainable Development Goal #4: Quality Education. I almost didn't go.

Not because I didn't want to — I did, very much. But because I wasn't sure I belonged there. The WEF is a gathering of heads of state, CEOs of global companies, Nobel laureates, and senior policymakers. I was a graduate student with a company I'd founded in college and a few years of consulting work. The imposter spiral was real.

What I found there surprised me.

What Education for Transformation Actually Means

The phrase "Education for Transformation" could mean a lot of things, and at Davos it meant all of them simultaneously, which made the conversations simultaneously rich and hard to pin down. But a few threads kept recurring across different sessions and conversations that I've continued to think about.

The first is the distinction between education that produces compliance and education that produces agency. Most formal education systems — globally, not just in developing countries — are optimized for the former. They teach students to absorb information, demonstrate comprehension, and progress through predefined sequences. This is not useless. But it is incomplete. The world those students graduate into rewards something different: the capacity to identify problems that don't have assigned solutions, form independent judgments in the presence of conflicting information, and act despite uncertainty.

The second thread was about access — but in a more interesting way than I expected. The conversation wasn't just about who gets education, but about what kind of education reaches whom. Quality is not evenly distributed. In many contexts, access to school exists, but access to education that develops critical thinking, creativity, and self-direction does not. The SDG framing pushes past enrollment numbers to ask harder questions about what's actually happening inside classrooms.

What I Brought to the Table

My perspective, for better or worse, was shaped by spending years working with people who had been failed by existing education systems — freelancers, remote workers, entrepreneurs who had learned to build things despite their education rather than because of it. The skills that made them effective — resourcefulness, tolerance for ambiguity, the ability to learn from failure — were not things they had been taught. They were things they had developed in spite of a system that discouraged them.

That observation landed differently in a room full of policymakers than I expected. The push-back I got was not that it was wrong, but that it was easier to say than to fix. Designing education systems that develop agency at scale, in resource-constrained environments, without the luxury of small class sizes and well-compensated teachers, is genuinely hard. I don't have a complete answer for that. But I think the framing matters: you can't fix a system you haven't correctly diagnosed.

What Surprised Me Most

The thing that surprised me most about Davos was not the scale of the gathering or the proximity to people I'd read about for years. It was how much genuine uncertainty the most accomplished people in the room were willing to admit to.

The conversations I valued most were not the ones where someone presented a polished framework for solving global education. They were the ones where someone said: "We tried this, and it didn't work, and we don't fully understand why." That kind of honesty, in a context designed for authority and polish, felt like the most important thing in the room.

It also reinforced something I had been building toward in my own work: the most transformative education — whether in a classroom, a company, or a community — is the kind that teaches people how to think, not what to think. And the best way to model that is to demonstrate it yourself, in public, even when the stakes are high.

What I Still Think About

The WEF conversation was useful partly because it was unresolved. I left without a clear answer to the central question — how do you design education systems that develop transformative capacity at the scale of billions of people — and I don't think that's a failure. Some questions are valuable not because they have answers, but because holding them keeps you honest about what you don't yet know.

The one thing I did leave with clearly: transformation is not a product of any single intervention. It accumulates — from teachers who ask good questions, from experiences that require genuine agency, from communities that model honest uncertainty. It is slow and distributed and very hard to measure. Which is exactly why systems keep optimizing for things that are faster and easier to count.
