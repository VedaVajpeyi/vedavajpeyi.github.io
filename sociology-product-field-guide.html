Sheet 01 — Quick diagnosis

Name the likely misread before the team names the wrong fix.

The fastest way to waste time in product is to start solving before the room agrees on what kind of problem it is. This sheet is for that first moment: what the team is calling the problem, what to check for, and which model to run first.

If the room cannot agree on the right column, do not discuss solutions yet. If you are torn between the funnel, social risk, and behavior system, start with the behavior system. It contains the diagnostic space the other two live inside.

Sheet 02 — The seven models

Run the seven models before you choose an intervention.

Each model is built to change the first question. Use them like a diagnostic stack. Most product failures become legible once one model snaps into focus, especially when the room keeps repeating words like useful, conversion, trust, or scale.

01 / The social adoption funnel

The dashboard starts too late.

Visible behavior is often decided during interpretation, comparison, and risk assessment, before the funnel logs a drop-off.

Spot it when

The feature is useful on paper, but public action still feels slow, awkward, or socially expensive.

Ask
  • Who is the audience for this act?
  • Do people like this user already do this?
  • What social risk shows up before action?
Try first

Lower exposure before lowering clicks: private-first starts, narrower audiences, and local proof.

02 / Social risk vs. functional value

Useful is not the same as adoptable.

Some acts create enough exposure, awkwardness, or status risk that functional value alone cannot carry them across the line.

Spot it when

The problem is real, the value is clear, and yet adoption still stalls because using the product says something socially costly.

Ask
  • What social cost comes attached to the act?
  • Who could judge or reinterpret this behavior?
  • Could we lower exposure or raise the status reward?
Try first

Reduce publicness, add trusted context, or make the first step easier to explain without embarrassment.

03 / The behavior system

Behavior sits at the overlap of person, culture, and system.

What looks like a product problem may live in norms or identity. What looks cultural may still be system-induced.

Spot it when

The team keeps arguing between user motivation and product friction without naming the social environment around the act.

Ask
  • What is broken at the person layer?
  • What is broken at the culture layer?
  • What is broken at the system layer?
Try first

Name one concrete blocker in each layer before deciding which team owns the fix.

04 / Structure before motivation

Before you motivate, check whether the choice is real.

Users are often constrained by time, money, permission, network position, or risk outside the product itself.

Spot it when

The desired behavior assumes slack, safety, or permission that your users may not have.

Ask
  • Can the user actually do this right now?
  • What has to be true in their life for this act to be available?
  • What cost sits outside the interface?
Try first

Name the one structural condition (time, money, permission, trust) that must be true before the behavior is genuinely available. Address that before touching the copy or the incentive.

05 / The metric is a social claim

Every metric hides a theory of value.

Metrics do not only measure. They privilege certain behaviors, users, and outcomes over others.

Spot it when

A number is improving while the product feels worse, narrower, harsher, or less trustworthy in practice.

Ask
  • What theory of value does this metric encode?
  • Who benefits from this definition?
  • What does this number make invisible?
Try first

Name one thing that should stay true about quality, trust, or fairness if this metric hits its target. Track it alongside the metric, not after.

06 / The trust stack

Trust breaks in layers, not all at once.

Functional polish cannot substitute for social proof, credible rules, reputation, or institutional legitimacy.

Spot it when

The interface is clear and the value prop is reasonable, but users still hesitate, verify, or quietly back away.

Ask
  • Is the break at functional trust, social proof, reputation, rules, or legitimacy?
  • Which layer is weakest right now?
  • What are users borrowing trust from instead?
Try first

Repair the lowest broken layer first: proof before polish, rules before scale, legitimacy before expansion.

07 / Value flow

Map who creates value, captures it, and absorbs the cost.

A product can look strategically healthy while routing labor and harm somewhere no dashboard is watching.

Spot it when

Convenience, scale, or growth appears to depend on invisible labor, externalized risk, or hidden harm.

Ask
  • Who creates the value?
  • Who captures the upside?
  • Who absorbs the off-dashboard cost?
Try first

Draw the value flow explicitly before approving a strategy that looks too clean to be true.

Sheet 03 — Use in the room

A meeting sequence for product reviews and postmortems.

When the logic of the roadmap is clear but behavior is still not following, ask these in order. The goal is not to sound clever. The goal is to stop solving the wrong class of problem.

Use one model at a time. Do not run the guide like a compliance checklist.

  1. Name the target behavior. What exactly do we need a user, team, or market to do?
  2. Name the audience around that behavior. Who is watching, judging, approving, or withholding legitimacy?
  3. Name the social risk. What could make the act feel awkward, exposing, low-status, or easy to misread?
  4. Run person, culture, system. Which layer is actually failing?
  5. Ask whether the choice is real. What structural conditions must exist before this act is genuinely available?
  6. Ask the time question. If this works for 12 months, what gets stronger, and what starts to decay? Does success eat itself?
  7. Interrogate the metric. What theory of value is our success measure enforcing?
  8. Check the org mirror. Does our team structure reproduce the same coordination problem we say users are facing?
  9. Map the value flow. Who creates, captures, and absorbs the cost of this system?
  10. Write the diagnosis in one sentence. “This is primarily a ____ problem, so the first move is ____.”
Do not do first
  • Do not rewrite onboarding before checking whether the act is socially safe.
  • Do not add incentives before checking whether the behavior is structurally available.
  • Do not celebrate the metric before checking what it is excluding or extracting.
  • Do not assume growth and trust are the same thing just because the line is moving up.
  • Do not run the seven models like a checklist and skip the one that gives you the uncomfortable answer.
Sheet 04 — Pattern library

Recognize the mechanism, not just the story.

Social adoption funnel

Facebook sharing decline

When too many audiences collapse into one stage, sharing declines before users can even fully explain why.

Lesson: Treat audience design as product architecture, not as a settings detail.

Behavior system

Nextdoor and thoughtful friction

When a platform removes ordinary social friction, bias moves faster. Reintroducing one pause changed the behavior itself.

Lesson: Some of the best product decisions make the harmful action harder, not the interface smoother.

Trust stack / emotional labor

Uber driver ratings

A five-star system looked like quality control from the platform's perspective. For drivers it became emotional labor under economic pressure.

Lesson: Ratings do not just measure service. They script the feeling work required to stay in the market.

Weak ties

TikTok's architecture

Distribution moved through weak ties rather than follower loyalty, which made discovery feel alive and let content jump clusters fast.

Lesson: If growth depends on novelty, design for bridges between clusters, not only reinforcement inside them.

Diffusion / trialability

Slack's team-by-team spread

Freemium made trial cheap, observability made adoption visible, and intact teams became the unit of spread instead of isolated users.

Lesson: Some products grow because the social unit of adoption is built directly into the product itself.

Exclusivity paradox

Clubhouse at scale

Its allure depended on exclusivity. As access widened, the very signal that made it desirable started to disappear.

Lesson: Some products lose part of their value by succeeding at scale.

Value flow

Ghost work behind AI

When a product feels magical, look for the labor it is hiding. The smoothness often lives downstream of someone else’s cost.

Lesson: If the product looks too elegant to map honestly, the value flow probably matters more than the feature list.

Metric is a social claim

Alexa and the inherited metric

A metric that looked like declining assistant engagement was actually hiding device abandonment. The wrong number kept the team solving the wrong problem.

Lesson: A number can improve while the behavior it was meant to represent reverses direction underneath it. The metric did not lie. It described the wrong thing with precision. Read the case study.

Authority and legitimacy

Aarogya Setu and trust

Mandate created velocity. Legitimacy still had to be earned. Scale did not solve the social problem; it exposed it faster.

Lesson: Authority can force uptake. It cannot manufacture trust.

If you cannot name the mechanism underneath the case, you are not ready to generalize from it.

Sheet 05 — Named laws

When the room needs a name for what it is seeing.

These are the fastest references in the guide because they let teams name the pattern in real time. Use them when something feels familiar, but nobody has language for it yet.

Org design

Conway's Law

The product will tend to mirror the communication structure of the team building it.

Reach for it when: architecture debates keep recurring no matter how clever the spec is.

Metrics

Goodhart's Law

When a measure becomes a target, people start optimizing the number instead of the reality it was meant to represent.

Reach for it when: the KPI is rising while quality, trust, or fairness seems to be falling.

Community scale

Dunbar's Number

Social intimacy has cognitive limits, which means groups do not become infinitely legible just because the tool allows them to grow.

Reach for it when: a once-useful channel, circle, or community feels noisy and thin after it gets bigger.

Responsibility

The Bystander Effect

When responsibility is diffuse, no one feels enough of it to act. Broadcasts create awareness; ownership creates response.

Reach for it when: generic reminders stall and action only happens once one person is clearly named.

Discovery

The Matthew Effect

Advantage compounds. Nodes that already have attention tend to get more of it.

Reach for it when: incumbents keep winning discovery and new voices never seem to break through.

Identity

Context Collapse

Too many audiences merge into one performance space, so expression flattens even when the feature still works.

Reach for it when: sharing gets safer, blander, or rarer as a network broadens.

Motivation

The Overjustification Effect

External rewards can crowd out the intrinsic reasons people had for participating in the first place.

Reach for it when: gamification makes the behavior shallower, more transactional, or less meaningful over time.

Sheet 06 — Keep reading

The shortest possible library, plus the essays that extend it.

Erving Goffman The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life

For public behavior, audience, impression management, and why products create stages whether they mean to or not.

danah boyd It's Complicated

For context collapse, networked publics, and the gap between what platforms make possible and what users can safely do.

Mark Granovetter The Strength of Weak Ties

For growth, diffusion, and why network structure often matters more than product cleverness.

Robert Putnam Bowling Alone

For bonding and bridging social capital, and why connection quality changes depending on what kind of tie a product is cultivating.

Pierre Bourdieu Distinction

For capital, class, taste, and why products often redistribute status instead of simply delivering utility.

Arlie Hochschild The Managed Heart

For emotional labor, service work, and what rating systems and marketplace products demand from people beyond the visible transaction.

Shoshana Zuboff The Age of Surveillance Capitalism

For data extraction, hidden value capture, and what looks different once the business model is treated as part of the product.

Everett Rogers Diffusion of Innovations

For adoption as a social process rather than an individual decision happening in a clean funnel.
