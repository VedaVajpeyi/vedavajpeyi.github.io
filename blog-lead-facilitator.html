Teaching Leadership: What 120 First-Years Taught Me

Veda Vajpeyi · Chicago Booth School of Business

In my second year at Chicago Booth, I was selected as one of 40 LEAD Facilitators — chosen by faculty to design and co-teach a full-credit leadership course to 120 incoming first-year MBA students. The program is called LEAD: Leadership Effectiveness and Development. It's one of the few required, credit-bearing leadership courses at a top business school.

I accepted because I thought I had something to teach. I was wrong — or at least, incomplete. What I didn't anticipate was how much the act of teaching would teach me.

The Setup

Being selected as a LEAD Facilitator is genuinely competitive. Faculty select 40 second-years from the full class based on demonstrated leadership, communication, and the capacity to hold difficult conversations with grace. The expectation is not that you have leadership figured out — it's that you're far enough along to be useful, and self-aware enough not to pretend otherwise.

The course covers the fundamentals of how people lead: self-awareness, influence, conflict, feedback, team dynamics, organizational power. These are not abstract concepts. They are things that 120 people — arriving from finance, consulting, tech, medicine, the military, nonprofits — have already experienced, often in contradictory ways.

My job was to create a space where those experiences could be examined honestly.

What I Expected to Happen

I expected the students to be the learners and me to be the teacher. I had the curriculum. I had been through the program myself. I had led teams across countries and time zones. I had founded a company. I assumed the learning would flow one direction.

It didn't.

What Actually Happened

The first session I facilitated, I watched a student — a former military officer — describe how she had once been forced to give an order she believed was wrong, and how she had done it anyway, and how she still thought about whether that was the right call. The room went quiet in a way that only happens when everyone is doing the same thing at the same time: thinking.

I had prepared a framework for that session. I did not use it.

What the students taught me, across a semester of two-hour sessions, was this: leadership is not a set of skills that transfers cleanly from one context to another. It is a continuous negotiation between your values, your constraints, the people in front of you, and the moment you're in. The frameworks are scaffolding. They help. But they are not the building.

I also learned that the most powerful thing a facilitator can do is not to demonstrate mastery — it is to model honest uncertainty. When I shared a moment where I had led badly, the room opened up. When I stayed in the role of expert, it closed.

The Feedback That Changed Something

Midway through the semester, a student told me in a one-on-one: "You're really good at asking the question that makes the room uncomfortable in the right way. But sometimes you answer it too fast. You rob us of the discomfort."

That landed. She was right. I had been so practiced at resolving intellectual tension that I was short-circuiting the exact process that produces real learning. Discomfort, held long enough, becomes insight. I had been tidying it up before it could do its work.

I spent the rest of the semester learning to sit in the silence longer.

What I Carry Forward

Teaching leadership at Booth was one of the most clarifying experiences of my career — not because it confirmed what I knew, but because it complicated it in productive ways.

I now believe that the best leaders are not the ones with the best answers. They're the ones who know which questions to ask, how to hold the space while others find their way to an answer, and when to let the discomfort do its work.

The 120 students I taught were not blank slates. They were doctors, veterans, engineers, analysts, founders — people who had already led, made hard calls, gotten things wrong, and kept going. My job was never to fill them with knowledge. It was to help them see what they already knew more clearly.

They did the same for me.
